### 1. Who are considered vulnerable populations, and what challenges might they face in digital spaces?

**Definition:** Vulnerable populations include individuals who may be at a higher risk of exploitation or harm, such as those with cognitive disabilities, neurodivergent traits (ADHD, Autism), or those facing significant life stressors.

**Challenges:** In digital spaces, these users often struggle with "dark patterns" (manipulative design), overwhelming sensory input (flashing lights/loud sounds), and complex navigation that taxes limited executive functioning.

### 2. What ethical considerations are important when designing or working with neurodivergent individuals?

**Cognitive Accessibility:** Avoid overwhelming User Experience (UX) by keeping interfaces clean and free of unnecessary distractions.

**Sensory Respect:** Ensure that software does not use auto-playing media or jarring transitions that could trigger sensory overload.

**Autonomy and Ethics:** Design features that empower the user rather than using addictive loops or shame-based notifications to drive engagement.

### 3. How can you make interactions and content more accessible for people with ADHD or Autism?

**Predictability:** Use consistent and predictable navigation so users don't have to "re-learn" how to use the app in every session.

**Reduced Cognitive Load:** Break information into small, digestible chunks and use simple, direct language.

**Visual Clarity:** Use high-contrast text and clear icons to support individuals who may have processing delays or visual sensitivities.

### 4. How can we support neurodivergent team members in a professional setting?

**Explicit Communication:** Be clear and direct in your requests; avoid "hinting" or using vague social cues that can be easily misunderstood.

**Respect Working Styles:** Recognize that some colleagues may work best in deep-work blocks or may need more time to process information before responding in meetings.

**Flexibility:** Allow for asynchronous communication (like Discord or Email) so teammates can engage when they have the highest mental bandwidth.

### 1. How can you adjust your communication style to be more inclusive of neurodivergent users and teammates?

**Directness and Clarity:** I will avoid vague "hinting" or idioms in my technical updates. Instead, I'll use clear, literal language to ensure my teammates know exactly what action is required from them.

**Asynchronous Respect:** I will lean on Discord or Email for non-urgent technical feedback, allowing neurodivergent colleagues to process information on their own terms without the pressure of an immediate verbal response.

**Detailed Context:** When reporting a security bug or requesting a change, I will provide the "Why" alongside the "How" to reduce the anxiety that can come with unexpected changes in workflow.

### 2. What are some common UX or communication pitfalls that might make Focus Bear less accessible?

**Cognitive Overload:** Using technical jargon or complex "security theatre" (like overly frequent, non-essential password resets) can frustrate users who already struggle with focus and motivation.

**Intrusive Notifications:** From a security perspective, sending too many high-alert system notifications can desensitize users with ADHD, leading them to ignore critical security warnings.

**Hidden Navigation:** If security settings are buried deep in menus, users with executive functioning challenges may struggle to keep their accounts secure.

### 3. What is one practical change you can make in your work to better support vulnerable populations?

**Accessible Security Documentation:** I will commit to writing my internal security guides and user-facing security alerts in Plain English, avoiding technical jargon and "security theatre" that can overwhelm or confuse users.

**The Goal:** By reducing technical "noise" and using simple, structured instructions, I can ensure that keeping Focus Bear secure is low-friction and high-success for every user and team member, regardless of their neurotype.
